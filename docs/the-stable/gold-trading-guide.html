So You Want to Trade Gold?

A Practical, Comprehensive Guide (Futures + ETFs)

Key Insight: Gold is a macro-financial asset disguised as a commodity. It doesn't behave like oil or wheat — it responds to real yields, FX, central banks, and capital flows.

1) Instruments & Specs

COMEX Gold Futures (GC)

  • Contract size: 100 troy oz
  • Tick: $0.10/oz → $10 per tick
  • $1.00 move = $100/contract
  • $10 move = $1,000/contract
  • Hours: Sun–Fri, 6:00pm–5:00pm ET (1-hr break daily)
  • Physically deliverable (most traders auto-roll before delivery)

Micro Gold (MGC): 10 oz contract, $1 per tick, more flexible sizing

Gold ETFs

  • GLD: 1/10 oz per share, physically backed, highly liquid
  • IAU/GLDM/SGOL: lower share price, cheaper fees, still physically backed
  • Leveraged/inverse ETFs: daily rebalanced, path-dependent, not for holding

Tax treatment (U.S.):

  • Futures: Section 1256, 60/40 blended rate
  • ETFs: Collectible treatment (up to 28%) if physically backed

2) How the Market Actually Works

  • OTC/LBMA (London): wholesale physical market, "Good Delivery" bars, twice-daily auctions
  • COMEX: futures/options for price discovery, linked to physical via EFP (Exchange for Physical)
  • ETFs: creation/redemption with authorized participants keeps ETF prices near NAV

3) Core Drivers of Gold

  • Real yields: the #1 driver; falling real yields = bullish
  • USD: stronger dollar usually pressures gold (though not always)
  • Monetary policy: Fed hikes/cuts, CPI/NFP/PCE data
  • Official demand: central bank reserve buying has been a strong tailwind
  • ETF flows: creations/redemptions amplify trends
  • Jewelry/physical demand: India (festivals), China (Shanghai premiums)
  • Geopolitics & risk events: safe-haven flows, stress hedging

4) Gold vs Other Commodities

Gold is not like oil, wheat, or copper. Its unique role in the financial system changes how it trades:

Monetary vs. consumable

  • Gold is primarily a monetary asset and store of value, not an industrial input
  • Oil, copper, wheat have end-user consumption driving supply/demand. Gold demand is largely investment, jewelry, and reserves

Supply elasticity

  • Annual mine output adds only ~1–2% to the above-ground stock
  • Contrast with oil or wheat, where yearly production swings directly shape price. Gold's stock-to-flow ratio is extremely high

Storage & carrying cost

  • Cheap to store relative to value, doesn't degrade
  • Gives it monetary longevity unlike soft commodities (spoil) or energy (high storage cost)

Correlation to macro, not cycles

  • Gold trades off real yields, FX, and policy
  • Base metals trade with global growth/industrial demand
  • Energy trades with supply shocks, OPEC policy, weather

Safe-haven premium

  • Gold often rallies because of financial stress, even if fundamentals don't justify it
  • Oil/copper usually fall in recessions or stress as demand collapses

ETF and central bank flows

  • No other commodity has the same scale of central bank buying or ETF demand as gold
  • This adds a financial layer of demand unique to gold

In short: Oil and grains are flow commodities (production vs consumption sets price). Gold is a stock commodity (massive above-ground reserves, demand anchored in financial perception and monetary regime). That's why it trades closer to currencies or bonds than traditional commodities.

5) Data & Dashboards Worth Tracking

  • Real yields (TIPS)
  • USD (DXY, key FX pairs)
  • ETF holdings (GLD/IAU/GLDM daily flows)
  • COT positioning
  • Shanghai premium vs London
  • COMEX warehouse stocks & EFP flows
  • Macro calendar: CPI, NFP, PCE, Fed

6) Trading Frameworks

For Futures

  • Event-driven trades around CPI/NFP/FOMC (treat like earnings)
  • Trend-following when macro regime is aligned (real yields falling)
  • Mean reversion between events (VWAP, prior-day value)
  • Risk: GC = $1,000 per $10 move per contract; size accordingly

For ETFs

  • Simpler execution, no leverage; options markets (esp. GLD) are liquid
  • Great for expressing medium-term thematic views (hedges, collars, spreads)
  • Beware collectible tax treatment
  • Leveraged/inverse ETFs only for short-term tactical plays

7) Pitfalls

  • Trading gold like oil/copper → you'll miss real drivers
  • Ignoring roll/basis in futures
  • Forgetting that "safe haven" narrative only works if positioning isn't already overloaded
  • Treating miners as 1:1 proxy (they carry equity risk and operational leverage)

Bottom Line

Gold is a macro-financial asset disguised as a commodity. It doesn't behave like oil or wheat — it responds to real yields, FX, central banks, and capital flows, with physical demand adding a background rhythm.

For futures traders, the key edge is knowing when macro catalysts align with positioning. For ETF traders, it's structuring trades around flows, options, and tax efficiency.
